The Hypothesis Scaffold

A workbook that walks a research question from a hunch to a falsifiable claim. You bring the curiosity; this page handles the structure.

Step One

Start with the rough idea.

Most research begins as a topic, not a question. Write what you’re curious about in your own words. We’ll sharpen it together.

Try one:
Watch out for A topic isn’t a hypothesis. “Social media” is a topic. “Heavier evening Instagram use predicts shorter sleep duration in undergraduates” is a hypothesis. The next steps will close that gap.

Step Two

Who is this actually about?

A hypothesis applies to a defined population. “People” is a flag, not an answer. Add age, role, place, or condition until the group is recognizable.

A useful test If a stranger read your population aloud, could they imagine three actual people who fit and three who don’t? If yes, you’re specific enough.
2 of 7

Step Three

Name the two things you think are linked.

The independent variable is what you suspect does the influencing. The dependent variable is what changes in response. If you can’t tell which is which, ask: “Which one comes first in time?”

Common trap Constructs vs. variables. “Stress” is a construct (a fuzzy idea). The variable is something specific like “self-reported stress on a 10-point scale.” Use the looser word here; we’ll pin it down in the next step.
3 of 7

Step Four

Turn each variable into something you can actually count.

This is where most hypotheses fall apart. If your variable can’t be measured, it can’t be tested. Pick a unit, an instrument, or a clear definition.

A shortcut For each variable ask: “What number, score, or category will I write down for each participant?” If you can answer, you’ve operationalized it.
4 of 7

Step Five

How will you investigate this?

This choice quietly governs which verbs you’re allowed to use. Manipulation earns causal language. Observation earns associational language.

Study design
Why this matters If you can’t randomly assign participants to high vs. low social media use, you can’t honestly say it “causes” anything. The honest verbs are predicts, is associated with, or correlates with. Saving you from a thesis revision later.
5 of 7

Step Six

Which way do you think it goes?

A directional hypothesis is bolder and more useful, but it commits you. A non-directional one is safer if prior evidence is mixed. Pick what you can defend.

Predicted relationship
Honest move If two solid studies disagree on direction, a non-directional hypothesis is the principled choice. Don’t pick a direction just because it sounds confident.
